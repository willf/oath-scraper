Oath ID 2747: Lycurgus, Against Leocrates, 80-82, (literary, Orat., 330)


  Date: 479
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Plataea   Location: unknown
 
  Swearer:
Greek hoplites (male, any adult age, free, various)
  Swearee:
Greek hoplites (male, any adult age, free, various)
 
  Proposed by:
unknown
 
  If taken: unknown
  If refused: unknown
  If kept: Polis free from sickness; polis unsacked; (land) bear (fruit); women bear children like to their parents; cattle bear young like to cattle (?)
  If broken: Polis afflicted with sickness; polis sacked; land barren; women bear monsters; cattle bear monsters (?)
 
  Taken: yes   True: unknown
  Impact: see remarks
  Consequences of breach: unknown
 
  Sanctifying features Blood sacrifices covered with shields (?)
 
  Statement: [That they will not hold life dearer than freedom, nor will they abandon their leaders whether they are alive or dead. That they will bury all allies killed in the battle. If they conquer the barbarians in war, they will not destroy any of the cities which have fought for Greece, but will consecrate a tenth of all those who sided with the barbarian. They will not rebuild a single one of the shrines which the barbarians have burnt and razed, but will allow them to remain for future generations as a memorial of the barbarians' impiety]
Οὐ ποιήσομαι περὶ πλείονος τὸ ζῆν τῆς ἐλευθερίας, οὐδ’ ἐγκαταλείψω τοὺς ἡγεμόνας οὔτε ζῶντας οὔτε ἀποθανόντας, ἀλλὰ τοὺς ἐν τῇ μάχῃ τελευτήσαντας τῶν συμμάχων ἅπαντας θάψω. καὶ κρατήσας τῷ πολέμῳ τοὺς βαρβάρους, τῶν μὲν μαχεσαμένων ὑπὲρ τῆς Ἑλλάδος πόλεων οὐδεμίαν ἀνάστατον ποιήσω, τὰς δὲ τὰ τοῦ βαρβάρου προελομένας ἁπάσας δεκατεύσω. καὶ τῶν ἱερῶν τῶν ἐμπρησθέντων καὶ καταβληθέντων ὑπὸ τῶν βαρβάρων οὐδὲν ἀνοικοδομήσω παντάπασιν, ἀλλ’ ὑπόμνημα τοῖς ἐπιγιγνομένοις ἐάσω καταλείπεσθαι τῆς τῶν βαρβάρων ἀσεβείας.
 
  Linguistic: ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: Lycurgus states that because no one who has who has broken his oath can deceive the gods, and if the perjurer does not suffer himself, his children and his family will suffer great misfortune (see oath id 2746), the Greeks exchanged a pledge at Plataea before taking their posts to fight against the power of Xerxes. He argues that the formula was not their own, but borrowed from the oath that is traditional among the Athenians.
Lycurgus then has the Plataea oath read to the jurors, and goes on to say that the Greeks "stood by this so strongly that they had the favour of the gods on their side to help them". He also (curiously) argues that the Athenians won the most renown at Plataea as a result.
It is remarkable that Lyc. should cite the oath in this form, and say the Athenians faithfully observed it, when he and all his audience know perfectly well that the Athenians had in fact rebuilt most of the destroyed sanctuaries, generally on the same site.
