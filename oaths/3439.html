Oath ID 3439: Demosthenes, Against Aristocrates, 67-8, (literary, Orat., 352)


  Date: 352
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Athens   Location: Areopagus
 
  Swearer:
Prosecutor (male, any adult age, free, Athenian)
  Swearee:
Presiding magistrate (male, mature/old, power-holder, Athenian)
 
  Proposed by:
Presiding magistrate
 
  If taken: He can prosecute for murder
  If refused: The case cannot proceed
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: Destruction of self, kin and household
 
  Taken: yes   True:
  Impact: n/a
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features The entrails of a boar, a ram and a bull
 
  Statement: [? That he knew well that the defendant had killed]
 
  Linguistic: omei, omnu, omwm, ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: Euthycles describes the oath sworn by prosecutors in a homicide trial and reminds the jurors that every man who brings an accusation must swear an oath, calling destruction upon himself, his kin and his household.
He also observes that whoever swears this oath must not treat it as a normal oath, but as one which no man swears for any other purpose. He notes that the swearer stands over the entrails of a boar, a ram and a bull, and they must have been slaughtered by the necessary officers and on the days appointed so that every requirement of solemnity has been met.
Euthycles argues that even then the person who has sworn this oath does not gain immediate credence; and if any falsehood is discovered he and his children and his kindred will be marked by his perjury.
"Does not gain immediate credence" presumably means that he is still expected to prove his case by argument.
For the oath of the defendant see oath id 3440.
