Oath ID 2745: Lycurgus, Against Leocrates, 79, (literary, Orat., 330)


  Date: 330
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Athens   Location: Stoa of the Basileus (repeated on Acropolis)
 
  Swearer:
Incoming archons (male, adolescent/mature, power-holder, Athenian)
  Swearee:
Presiding magistrate (male, mature/old, power-holder, Athenian)
 
  Proposed by:
Presiding magistrate
 
  If taken: They can hold office
  If refused: They cannot hold office
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: They will dedicate a golden statue
 
  Taken: yes   True: unknown
  Impact: see remarks
  Consequences of breach: unknown
 
  Sanctifying features Presence of the lithos on which the sacrificial victims are cut up
 
  Statement: [That they will act in office justly and according to the laws, and not take gifts for performing their official duties, and if they do take any gift, they will dedicate a golden (life-size) statue (at Delphi)]
 
  Linguistic: ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: Lycurgus argues that the oath is the force that holds democracy together. He cites three oaths: the oath sworn by archons, the oath sworn by judges i.e. the Heliastic oath, and the oath of the private citizens i.e. the ephebic oath. He says that each of these men gives this pledge, and rightly.
According to Arist. Ath.Pol. 55.5, the archons " go to the stone on which the severed parts (of sacrificial victims) are, the same stone on which the arbitrators swear when they give their decisions and witnesses when they swear an oath to excuse them from testifying; and they go up on to it and swear to act in office justly and according to the laws, and not to take gifts for performing their official duties, and if they do take any gift, to dedicate a golden (life-size) statue (at Delphi: Plut. Sol. 25.3). Having sworn, they go from there to the Acropolis and take the same oath again there, and after that they enter office."
The stone was in front of the Stoa Basileios
See also oath id 2743 and 2744.
For the oath of the archon see oath id 1753.
