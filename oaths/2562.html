  Remarks: Aeschines observes that the same argument always works with juries: "Gentlemen of the jury, the demesmen with their vote rejected this man on oath, though nobody in the world accused him or bore witness against him; they voted on the basis of their knowledge".
This refers to a particular occasion - the revision of the deme registers in 346/5. The oath of the demesmen is mentioned in Dem. 57.8-9; it was taken at the revision meeting of the deme, and presumably it included an undertaking to decide every case fairly, etc., much as in the dicastic oath. Euxitheus, the speaker, complains that his enemies deleted from this oath the phrases, which of course formed part of the dicastic oath, about voting "in accordance with the most just opinion, and not from favour or enmity" (Dem. 57.63); but he does not say that by doing so they were breaking the law, and it may well be that the oath prescribed by decree for the demesmen on this occasion was shorter and simpler than that prescribed for jurors - in other words, that the phrases mentioned were deleted from the oath, not by Eubulides and his cronies at deme level, but by the Assembly when it set up the review procedure.
Incidentally, Dem. 57.44 and 53 seem to show that when a man was struck off the deme register and afterwards appealed to a dicastic court, any witnesses he brought forward had to confirm their testimony by oath. The logic of this, presumably, is that the demesmen's decision was made on oath and should not be overturned on the basis of statements not made on oath.
