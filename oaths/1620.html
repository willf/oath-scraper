  Remarks: Aristotle is not making this statement in his own person; the speaking voice here is that of a hypothetical defender of the thesis that the majority have the right to rule; in other words, we have here the familiar use of νὴ Δία to introduce an imaginary objector’s words. The argument (1281a14-21) runs thus, if one puts it, so to speak, into Socratic form:

Aristotle: Is it just for the poor, if they are the majority in a city, to seize and redistribute the wealth of the rich?
Majoritarian: Yes (by Zeus), because that was the decision of the rightful rulers, and the decision of the rightful rulers is [apparently by definition] just.
Aristotle: Is justice a virtue?
Majoritarian: Of course.
Aristotle: Can virtue ever ruin its possessor?
Majoritarian: No.
Aristotle: Do you think the city is likely to survive as a cohesive community, if the wealth of the rich is seized in this way?
Majoritarian: Quite likely not.
Aristotle: And will its destruction be the ruin of rich and poor alike?
Majoritarian: I suppose so.
Aristotle: But in that case, according to your own statement, the poor, in causing their own ruin, cannot have been acting justly?

The Statement, taken literally, seems to assume what has to be proved (viz. that the action is just), but this is probably merely due to Aristotle's compressed style. The chapter proceeds to consider various other possible answers to the question “who has the right to rule?”, and by the end of it we have found that any ruling authority (whether the many, the few, the one, or even the law) may commit unjust acts. It must follow (though, typically, Aristotle’s text does not spell this out) that the proposition “The decision of the rightful rulers is just” is false; in other words, that no person, group or institution can be said to have the right to rule (to be kyrion, in Aristotle’s terminology) if by that is meant the right to enforce their will, whatever it may be, on the community as a whole.
