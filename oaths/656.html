Oath ID 656: Aristophanes, Birds, 630-635, (literary, Comic., 414)


  Date: 414
  Reference type: Oath
  State: unknown   Location: Far from Athens
 
  Swearer:
Chorus (male) (n/a, mature, free, n/a)
  Swearee:
Peisetaerus (male, old, free, Athenian)
 
  Proposed by:
n/a
 
  If taken: n/a
  If refused: n/a
  If kept: No longer will the gods abuse the birds' sceptre
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: yes
  Impact: see remarks
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features none
 
  Statement: [That if Peisetaerus "joins thoughts" with the birds, justly, sincerely, piously to attack the gods, in harmony of thought with the birds, that the gods will not be abusing their sceptre for much longer]
κατώμοσα,
ἢν σὺ παρ’ ἐμὲ θέμενος ὁμόφρο-
νας λόγους δίκαιος ἄδολος
ὅσιος ἐπὶ θεοὺς ἴῃς,
ἐμοὶ φρονῶν ξυνῳδά, μὴ
πολὺν χρόνον θεοὺς ἔτι
σκῆπτρα τἀμὰ τρίψειν.
 
  Linguistic: wmos
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: This is a parody of a real oath. Line 631 is a comic adaptation of a military phrase meaning to join forces with someone, hence Sommerstein's translation "join thoughts with me". As Sommerstein notes ad 632, of the terms used the first two appear in treaties and oaths of peace. The third term chosen here "results in an attack on the gods being paradoxically described as "pious"."; see the commentaries of both Sommerstein and Dunbar for further detail.
