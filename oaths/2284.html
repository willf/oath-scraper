  Statement: [That Socrates seems to be a truly noble man]
Νὴ τὴν Ἥραν, ὦ Σώκρατες, καλός γε κἀγαθὸς δοκεῖς μοι ἄνθρωπος εἶναι.
 
  Linguistic: nh thn
 
  God(s) invoked:
Hera    
 
  Remarks: Swearing by Hera is unusual. Only Socrates does it repeatedly. Both Callias and Hermogenes swear by Hera in this dialogue (see oath ids 2256 and 2281 with cross references for Socrates' oaths by Hera). Callias and Hermogenes are from the same deme as Socrates, which may explain their use of this oath. The reference books, such as LGPN, give Lycon’s deme as Thorikos. This is known from schol. Pl. Apol. 23e, which refers, of course, to the Lycon who was one of Socrates’ accusers. The present passage, with its praise of Socrates, seems to prove conclusively that the accuser and the father of Autolycus were two different people; the only thing they are known to have in common is that they were apparently both accused in comedy of being foreigners (Eupolis fr. 61, Metagenes fr. 10). Another argument against identifying them is that if Autolycus was born in the late 440s, his father would have had to be at least as old as Socrates, in which case it would be surprising to find him in 399 as the third man in a prosecution team of three.
