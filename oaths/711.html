Oath ID 711: Aristophanes, Thesmophoriazusae, 269-276, (literary, Comic., 411)


  Date: 411
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Athens   Location: Outside Agathon's house
 
  Swearer:
Euripides (male, old, free, Athenian)
  Swearee:
Inlaw (male, old, free, Athenian)
 
  Proposed by:
Inlaw
 
  If taken: The Inlaw will put on a woman's voice
  If refused: The Inlaw will not put on a woman's voice
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: yes
  Impact: The Inlaw finally goes off to infiltrate the Thesmophoria
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features none
 
  Statement: [That he will save the Inlaw by hook or by crook if he finds himself in trouble]
Μὰ τὸν Ἀπόλλω οὔκ, ἤν γε μὴ
ὀμόσῃς ἐμοί—
{ΕΥ.} Τί χρῆμα;
{ΚΗ.} συσσώσειν ἐμὲ
πάσαις τέχναις, ἤν μοί τι περιπίπτῃ κακόν.
{ΕΥ.} Ὄμνυμι τοίνυν αἰθέρ’, οἴκησιν Διός.
{ΚΗ.} Τί μᾶλλον ἢ τὴν Ἱπποκράτους ξυνοικίαν;
{ΕΥ.} Ὄμνυμι τοίνυν πάντας ἄρδην τοὺς θεούς.
{ΚΗ.} Μέμνησο τοίνυν ταῦθ’, ὅτι ἡ φρὴν ὤμοσεν,
ἡ γλῶττα δ’ οὐκ ὀμώμοκ’, οὐδ’ ὥρκωσ’ ἐγώ.
 
  Linguistic: omnu, omos, omwm, wmos, wrk
 
  God(s) invoked:
Aither    
All the gods    
 
  Remarks: Euripides' initial oath by Aither (a quotation from his own play Melanippe the Wise - see oath id 194) is rejected by the Inlaw, who evidently does not regard Aither as a real god, and Euripides substitutes a more conventional oath by "all the gods". Being a comedy, of course, the Inlaw does get into trouble and Euripides upholds his oath by coming to his aid. In lines 275-276, the Inlaw reminds Euripides that his "heart" has sworn and not his "tongue". This, of course, is a reference to the famous line 612 from Euripides' Hippolytus, where the title character is bound by an oath of silence- see oath id 125; cf. also oath ids 376 and 514.
