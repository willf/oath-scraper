  If kept: May she drink from the wine-cup
  If broken: May the wine-cup be filled with water
 
  Taken: yes   True: yes
  Impact: The sex strike is ensured and peace is enabled
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features The "slaughter" of a jar of Thracian wine over a large, black-based cup
 
  Statement: [That no man whatever, neither lover nor husband shall come near her with his cock up, and that she will pass her life at home, pure and chaste in make-up and saffron gown so that her husband may be greatly inflamed with desire for her, and that she will never yield herself to her husband of her own free will. And if he should force her against her will, she will submit grudgingly and not thrust back. She will not raise her Persian slippers ceilingwards. She will not stand in the lioness-on-a-cheesegrater position]

  Remarks: Lysistrata calls upon Peitho and the Cup of Amity to receive their oath-sacrifice. The women collectively invoke Zeus when they swear agreement at 237. As Sommerstein notes ad 203, the Cup of Amity was "a cup by the sharing of which two or more persons pledge friendship to one another". The "if kept" and "if broken" terms and conditions of the oath reflect the characteristic stereotype of women liking their wine neat (see further Sommerstein ad 197). It is noteworthy that the cup of wine used in the oath ritual is brought on by a Scythian; cf. Herodotus 4.70 (id 1327) for details of Scythian oath rituals. Note also the similar sanctifying features of slaughter and blood caught in a shield in A. Seven Against Thebes, see oath id 198.
