  Statement: [That Cyrus would have proved himself an excellent leader had he lived]

  Remarks: The Cyrus mentioned here is Cyrus the Younger, confusing after a mention of Cyrus the Great (in 4.16). Sommerstein (in correspondence) notes though that the particle-combination καὶ … γε “and also” shows that the two men are in fact clearly distinguished even though they are referred to by the same name. There is a general tendency in antiquity, to us surprising, to refer casually to different persons of the same name in close proximity and rely on the context to distinguish between them; this tendency is particularly noticeable in speeches in inheritance lawsuits (such as those of Isaeus), where translators are forced to insert Roman numerals in brackets to make it clear whether the speaker is talking about (say) Dicaeogenes I, II or III when the speaker himself just says Dicaeogenes, every time. Cf. also, for example, Ar. Acharnians 46-51 where a character gives his name as Amphitheus, then says “Amphitheus was the son of Demeter and Triptolemus”, and then gives a genealogy which makes him the great-grandson of this earlier Amphitheus – a passage which can’t be translated without amplification, e.g. speaking of “the first Amphitheus” or “the original Amphitheus.” In our passage it would be easy for a listener or reader to distinguish between the Cyrus of 4.16, who is described as having (actually) been an excellent king, and the Cyrus of 4.18 of whom it can only be said that he would have been one had he lived to reign.
