  Statement: [That tyrants are not more loved than ordinary men because they are more often forced to engage in actions that make men hated]
Ὅτι νὴ Δί’, ἔφη, ὦ Σιμωνίδη, καὶ ἐξ ὧν ἀπεχθάνονται ἄνθρωποι, ἡμᾶς πολὺ πλείω τῶν ἰδιωτῶν ἀνάγκη ἐστὶ πραγματεύεσθαι.
 
  Linguistic: nh
 
  God(s) invoked:
Zeus    
 
  Remarks: Hiero is responding to Simonides' observation that high rank itself is a most striking embellishment to the person which casts a shadow over anything repulsive in him and highlights his best features. Because of this, Simonides argues, services rendered by rulers are rewarded with greater gratitude, and that, "When you have the power to give more, it is natural that you should be more deeply loved than private citizens".
Simonides has asked, literally, "How can it not be the case that a tyrant, who can confer such great benefits and gifts on his subjects, will be more loved than an ordinary man can be?" And Hiero replies "Because, by Zeus, we also have to do many more of the things that cause a man to be hated." Hence the statement is "That a tyrant, despite the benefits and gifts he can confer on his subjects, will not be greatly loved, because he will also be forced to do many things that make him hated."
