  Remarks: This is a complex discussion concerning oath challenges (cf. oath id 1728) and how to manipulate them. There are four possibilities. 1) to tender an oath and take one. 2) to do neither of these things. 3) to tender an oath and not take one, or 4) to take but not tender. In fact there are two kinds of oath challenges- those volunteered, and those tendered. There are real reasons for not wanting the other side to swear- men are too ready to commit perjury, and if the opponent does not swear, the jury will probably condemn him. One can trust jurors more than opponents. Aristotle tries to find valid arguments for refusing an opponent's oath-challenge (e.g. that the oath is only to be taken with a view to money), but in reality no one could do this convincingly, and litigants who have refused such a challenge normally avoid discussing the matter in their speeches. There follows advice regarding what to say when taking an oath or issuing an oath challenge. Taking an oath, one may claim confidence in oneself, but not in one's opponent, and one may argue that the only fair thing is for the impious man to tender the oath and the pious man to take it, and that it would be terrible to refuse an oath, while demanding that the judges take one before giving their verdict. This last point should also be mentioned on tendering oaths, along with a declaration of faith in the gods as judges. Aristotle also argues that if one has already taken an oath which contradicts the present one, it can be argued that the previous oath was taken by error or under compulsion; whereas if it is the opponent who has taken an oath which he now contradicts, it can be said that one who does not abide by what he swore subverts everything, for this is the reason the dikasts take an oath before applying the laws, and the opponent can be accused of demanding the judges' adherence to their oaths while himself not abiding by his.
