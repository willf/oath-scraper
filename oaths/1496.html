  Remarks: According to Herodotus, when King Leotychides went to Athens to organise the release of Aeginetan hostages left there on trust by Cleomenes, the Athenians refused to hand them over.
Leotychides then warned them about the fate of a certain Spartan, Glaucus son of Epicydes, who lived three generations earlier.
Glaucus had a reputation for justice such that a man from Miletus came to give him half his property (recently converted into silver) for safe-keeping. If Glaucus received the money he would take and keep tokens, and restore the money to any person who came with like tokens and demanded the money back.
Glaucus received the trust, and after a long time the sons of the Milesian came to Sparta, showed Glaucus the tokens, and demanded the money back. Glaucus claimed that he could not yet remember the matter, and promised that if they returned in four months he would settle the matter one way or another.
Glaucus then travelled to Delphi to consult the oracle and asked whether he should steal the money "by an oath" (ὅρκῳ).
The priestess threatened him with the following verses:
"For now, Glaucus son of Epicydes, you have an immediate profit; to win by oath (ὅρκῳ νικῆσαι) and to steal the money; swear (ὄμνυ), since death awaits even the man who swears well (εὔορκον μένει ἄνδρα); but Horkos has a child with no name, nor hands, nor feet, but swift in pursuit, until he has in his grasp all a man's offspring and household, which he destroys. But the offspring of the man who swears well shall afterwards be better".
When Glaucus heard this, he asked the god to forgive him for what he had suggested. But the Pythia responded that to tempt the god and to do the deed were the same thing.
Glaucus then sent for the Milesians and returned the money to them.
Leotychides then goes on to warn the Athenians that there are no descendants of Glaucus, nor any household that bears his name at Sparta, and warns them that the only good thing to do concerning a trust is to restore it on demand.
