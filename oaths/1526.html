Oath ID 1526: Hippocrates, Oath, Oath, (literary, Med., 425-322)


  Date: 425 to 322
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Any state   Location: At medical school
 
  Swearer:
Students (male, mature/old, free, Greek)
  Swearee:
Prospective teachers (male, mature/old, free, Greek)
 
  Proposed by:
Hippocrates?
 
  If taken: Student will be allowed to learn medicine
  If refused: Student will not be allowed to learn medicine
  If kept: He will enjoy the benefits both of his life and his skill and be held in good repute among all forever
  If broken: He will not enjoy the benefits of his life and his skill and will not be held in good repute
 
  Taken: yes   True: unknown
  Impact: Protects the interests of the patient
  Consequences of breach: unknown
 
  Sanctifying features unknown
 
  Statement: [That the swearer will regard his teacher as equal to his parents, and share his livelihood with the teacher and give him a share when he is in need of necessities, and that he will judge his teacher's offspring as equal to his siblings and that he will teach them his skill should they desire to learn it, without fee and written covenant, and that he will give a share of all his learning to his own sons and the sons of his teacher and to the pupils who have made a written contract with him and sworn the medic's oath, but will not teach his skill to any other. He will use regimens for the benefit of the ill in accordance with his ability and judgement and will keep the ill from harm or injustice. He will not give a drug that is deadly to anyone if asked for it, nor will he suggest any such thing, nor give a woman a destructive pessary. He will guard his life and his skill in a pure and holy manner. He will not cut patients, not even those suffering from stone, but will cede this to men who are practitioners of this activity. Into as many houses as he enters, he will go for the benefit of the ill, while being far from all voluntary and destructive injustice, especially from sexual acts both upon women's bodies and upon men's, both of the free and of slaves. About whatever he may see or hear in treatment, or even without treatment, in the life of human beings, he will remain silent, holding such things unutterable (sacred, not to be divulged)]
