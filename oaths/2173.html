Oath ID 2173: Xenophon, Anabasis, 3.21-23, (literary, Hist., ca.370-355)


  Date: 401
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Persia   Location: Assyria
 
  Swearer:
Tissaphernes (male, mature, power-holder, Persian)
The brother of the King's wife (male, mature, power-holder, Persian)
  Swearee:
Greek generals (male, mature, power-holder, various)
Greek lochagoi (male, mature, power-holder, various)
 
  Proposed by:
Tissaphernes
 
  If taken: The treaty would be formalised
  If refused: There would not be a treaty
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: no
  Impact: see remarks
  Consequences of breach: see remarks
 
  Sanctifying features Clasping of right hands
 
  Statement: [That the territory the Greeks pass through shall be friendly, and that they shall lead the Ten Thousand back to Greece without treachery, providing them with a market; and wherever they do not furnish the Ten Thousand with a market they will allow them to take provisions from the country]
 
  Linguistic: ork, wrk
 
  God(s) invoked:
 
  Remarks: When Xenophon addresses the lochagoi of Proxenus the Boeotian, after mentioning that he used to be envy the Persians and fear the truce they had made with them, Xenophon goes on to say, "But seeing that their own act has put an end to the truce, the end has likewise come, in my opinion, both of their arrogance and of our embarrassment. For now all these good things are offered as prizes for whichever of the two parties shall prove to be the braver men; and the judges of the contest are the gods, who, in all likelihood, will be on our side. For our enemies have sworn falsely by them, while we, with abundant possessions before our eyes, have steadfastly kept our hands therefrom because of our oaths by the gods; hence we, I think, can go into the contest with far greater confidence than can our enemies.
Besides, we have bodies more capable than theirs of bearing cold and heat and toil, and we likewise, by the blessing of the gods, have better souls; and these men are more liable than we to be wounded and killed, if the gods again, as on that former day, grant us victory."
For other references to the treaty between the Ten Thousand and the Persians, see also oath id 2098.
