Oath ID 443: IG II(2), 111, 27-41, (inscriptional, Public, 363/2)


  Date: 376-375 or before 363/2 (376 to 362)
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Ceos   Location: Iulis
 
  Swearer:
Iulietans (male, any adult age, free, Iulietan)
Carthaeans (male, any adult age, free, Carthaean)
  Swearee:
Chabrias (male, mature, power-holder, Athenian)
 
  Proposed by:
unknown
 
  If taken: Their alliance would be formalised
  If refused: There would not be an alliance
  If kept: Many good things (?)
  If broken: ill (?)
 
  Taken: yes   True: more complex
  Impact: unknown
  Consequences of breach: unknown
 
  Sanctifying features unknown
 
  Statement: [? That they would be an ally of the Athenians and the allies, and would not defect from the Athenians and the allies themselves, nor would they allow another as far as possible. They would make all private and public lawsuits against Athenians subject to appeal in accordance with the agreement, as many as are for more than one hundred drachmas. If anyone dares to wrong those of the Ceans who have returned, or the Athenians or any of the allies, contrary to the oaths and the agreement, they would not allow him by any craft or contrivance, but would go in support with all their strength as far as possible]
 
  Linguistic: ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: This inscription refers to an alliance between Athens and the poleis on Ceos.
The exact statement is unknown. However, it must have been similar to the new agreement sworn by the Ceans in 363/2 (see oath id 424).
The text of the decree makes it clear that several Iulietans had been convicted of violating their oaths and were condemned to death. They subsequently returned to Ceos and overturned the stelai on which the agreement and the names of those who had violated the oath were written. They went on to attack the so-called friends of the Athenians whom the Athenian demos had reinstalled at Iulis, executing some, condemning others to death, and confiscating the property of other including Satyrides, Timoxenus and Miltiades, all in violation of the oath and agreement. These latter three victims were attacked because they had spoken against a certain Antipater when the Athenian boule had condemned him to death for killing Aeson the Athenian proxenos. The penalty for their crimes was exile from Ceos and Athens and the confiscation of their property. The citizens of Carthaea on Ceos were praised for abiding by their oaths.
See also oath id 422, 424, 427, 520, 521.
