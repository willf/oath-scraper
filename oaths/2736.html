Oath ID 2736: Plato, Laws, 954a4-8, (literary, Phil., 357-347)

  State: Magnesia   Location: unknown

  Statement: [That he expects to find the object he is searching for]
Φωρᾶν δὲ ἂν ἐθέλῃ τις παρ’ ὁτῳοῦν, γυμνὸς ἢ χιτωνίσκον ἔχων ἄζωστος, προομόσας τοὺς νομίμους θεοὺς ἦ μὴν ἐλπίζειν εὑρήσειν, οὕτω φωρᾶν·
 
  Linguistic: omos
 
  God(s) invoked:
The gods    
 
  Remarks: It is stated here that a man can search the premises of another man provided that he strips to his chiton and removes his girdle and then takes this oath by the "appointed gods". If, having done this, he is denied access to the house, including things "both sealed and unsealed", he should take legal proceedings against the man who obstructed him. Should this man be found guilty, he is liable to pay the double value of the item originally sought. The law that one could search another’s house for stolen goods, but only if one first removed one’s outer garment, also existed at Athens, cf. Ar. Clouds 498-9. The object of the divestment rule was to prevent not only theft but also the smuggling in and planting of articles on the premises in order to then make false allegations. We do not know whether Athenian law prescribed an oath to be taken before making such a search.
