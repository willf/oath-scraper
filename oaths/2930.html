Oath ID 2930: Plato, Phaedrus, 236d-e, (literary, Phil., 375-355)


  Date: fictional time
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Athens   Location: In the countryside
 
  Swearer:
Phaedrus (male, mature, free, Athenian)
  Swearee:
Socrates (male, old, free, Athenian)
 
  Proposed by:
n/a
 
  If taken: n/a
  If refused: n/a
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: yes
  Impact: Socrates feels compelled to give his speech
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features unknown
 
  Statement: [That unless Socrates delivers his speech in the very presence of the plane tree, Phaedrus will never again report any other speech by any other author]
ὁ δέ μοι λόγος ὅρκος ἔσται. ὄμνυμι γάρ σοι—τίνα μέντοι, τίνα θεῶν; ἢ βού λει τὴν πλάτανον ταυτηνί; —ἦ μήν, ἐάν μοι μὴ εἴπῃς τὸν λόγον ἐναντίον αὐτῆς ταύτης, μηδέποτέ σοι ἕτερον λόγον μηδένα μηδενὸς μήτε ἐπιδείξειν μήτε ἐξαγγελεῖν.
 
  Linguistic: omnu, ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
see remarks    
 
  Remarks: Phaedrus wonders which of the gods to swear by and eventually decides to invoke the plane tree as witness. If it fails to hear Socrates’ speech, it will know that it must hold Phaedrus to his promise and punish him for any breach of it. Trees, after all, could have indwelling spirits (Dryads or Hamadryads); one tree, to be mentioned later in the Phaedrus (275b), was the first prophet; and the whole location is sacred to the Nymphs (230b). Cf. oath id 2931.
