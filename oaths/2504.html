  Remarks: If this chapter is genuine, it must have been written later than 367 (see below). That does not necessarily entail that the whole work was written that late; this chapter, which ruins the ending of the work, must (if it is by Xenophon) be what one might call a "grumbling appendix" rather like chapter 14 of the Lakedaimonion Politeia. Here Xenophon remarks on the decline of morals in Persia after Cyrus' death, giving the example of the satrap Rheomithres who betrays his wife and children and the children of his friends, leaving them as hostages with the king of Egypt. He apparently assisted the satrap Ariobarzanes (who is also mentioned in this passage) in revolting against Artaxerxes II in 367 B.C. Ariobarzanes sent Rheomithres to Tachos, king of Egypt, for help. Rheomithres received troops and money through which he was able to make peace with the king of Persia, and has no concern for the hostages he left in Egypt. Xenophon complains that he has broken 'the greatest oaths', but it is not clear exactly what the oaths were. Perhaps they were the generic oaths sworn by Persians and their king since those of Cyrus and his subjects (see oath ids 2500 and 2501). Betrayal of fellow Persians would certainly count as a violation of this oath. Alternatively he may be referring to oaths exchanged between Rheomithres and Ariobarzanes themselves as part of their conspiracy. Xenophon further complains that men capable of such actions seem only to have the highest honours heaped on them.
