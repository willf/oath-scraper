Oath ID 1802: Aristotle, Athenaion Politeia, 49.2.7-11, (literary, Phil., 335-322)

  Statement: [That they are physically or financially unable to serve]

  Remarks: Currently-serving cavalrymen or new deme members up for cavalry service were both able to decline duty by swearing to physical or financial incapacity. The procedure, as carefully described, is:
(a) The katalogeis draw up the provisional list of cavalrymen.
(b) They pass it to the hipparchs and phylarchs who …
(c)… bring it before the council, who open the sealed document.
(d) The council erase the names of those who swear that they are not physically fit to serve.
(e) Those remaining on the list are then summoned before the council.
(f) Anyone who then swears that he is physically or financially unable to serve is exempted.
(g) For each man still regarded as liable, the council vote on whether he is a suitable person to serve on horseback.
It thus looks as though a claim of physical unfitness, once entered, remained on the record, and anyone who had made such a claim in one year would automatically be exempted in future years, without having to appear before the council. Claims of financial incapacity, on the other hand, had to be publicly renewed each year: a man’s material fortunes could recover, whereas his physical fitness was less likely to improve with the years (especially when it came to cavalry service, which was very much a young man’s game).
