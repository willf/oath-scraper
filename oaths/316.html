Oath ID 316: Homer, Iliad, 3.73-75, (literary, Epic., date uncertain)


  Date: mythical time
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Troy   Location: On the battle plain
 
  Swearer:
Greek army (male, any adult age, free, various Greek)
Trojan army (male, any adult age, free, Trojan)
  Swearee:
Greek army (male, any adult age, free, various Greek)
Trojan army (male, any adult age, free, Trojan)
 
  Proposed by:
Paris
Hector
 
  If taken: War will be determined by single combat between Paris and Menelaus
  If refused: War continues as it is
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: Let their brains be spilled on the ground and those of their sons, and let their wives be the spoils of others
 
  Taken: yes   True: more complex
  Impact: peace
  Consequences of breach: war resumes
 
  Sanctifying features Sheep sacrificed, wine poured, hands washed with water, fruit brought
 
  Statement: [That the Greeks and the Trojans will stop fighting, and that Menelaus and Paris will engage in single combat over Helen and her possessions]
 
  Linguistic: ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
Zeus Epithet(s) father, most honoured, greatest
Helios Epithet(s) all-seeing, all-hearing
Rivers    
Gaia    
Nether Avengers against dead perjurers    
All the immortal gods    
 
  Remarks: This oath is recounted in detail at oath id 318. Here Paris suggests the oath to Hector who then proposes it to Menelaus (3.73 = 3.94, cf. oath id 317). The oath is true in that peace is made, but the oath is subsequently broken by the Trojan ally Pandarus through the intervention of Athena (see also oath ids 322-326). Aristotle discusses both the oath and the manner in which it is broken in Aporematon Homericon (see oath ids 1999, 2000, 2002). In Plato's Republic, the passage is rejected as unsuitable material for the members of the ideal state because it presents the gods as instigating the violation of the oaths (see oath id 2540).
