  Remarks: Sommerstein offers the following interpretation of this passage: X calls Callicles to the stand and asks him to testify, and (by prior collusive arrangement, of course) he says "No, I won’t, because I tried to stop this man [X’s opponent] doing this [what X has accused him of]." The italicized statement is (we are to understand) false, but because it has been made, not as part of a witness statement, but in the process of refusing to make a witness statement (en apophasei), Callicles is not liable to be sued for giving false testimony since he has not given any testimony at all!! Of course he had no business making a statement like that to the jury if he wasn’t actually a witness; but he couldn’t help it slipping out … . In Athens, at the time when Anaximenes was writing, X would have had to prepare a written statement of evidence for Callicles to confirm, and if Callicles refused to confirm it, he would have had to swear that he did not know the statement to be true; but there would of course be no difficulty in composing a statement about which the (non-)witness could truthfully take such an oath. To take a concrete (if slightly different – and purely imaginary) example:

X falsely accuses Y of beating his wife.
X prepares a witness statement for Z: “Y beats his wife regularly.”
Z, called to the stand, says “No, I can’t testify to that; actually Y has stopped beating her.”
Z swears that he does not know the prepared statement to be true.

X has thus got Z to tell the jury, falsely, that Y has in the past beaten his wife (and is thus guilty as charged) without either committing perjury or giving false testimony.
