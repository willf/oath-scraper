  Remarks: Alcibiades, in his eulogy of Socrates, suggests that were he not afraid that the others would tell him he were drunk, he would swear an oath to the effect of the statement. However, the oath is never taken, and mention of it seems to serve a purely rhetorical purpose. The rhetorical purpose is presumably to affirm very strongly (i) that the statement is true and (ii) that the effect that Socrates has on Alcibiades is so extraordinary that if Alcibiades were to describe it in detail, no one would believe him even if he made his statement on oath. There may also be, on Plato’s part, an allusion back to what Pausanias said about lovers’ oaths (see oath id 2925). That Alcibiades is in fact very drunk will have been as obvious to those present as it is to the reader; it is not clear whether we are to take him as being unaware of this (as people in that state often are) or as only pretending to be unaware of it.
