Hera?    
 
  Remarks: Cyrus does eventually pluck up the courage to broach the subject with his grandfather, but the fear of confronting his grandfather about putting himself (and the other boys) in danger, after he has been warned against this, is clearly genuine. The OCT is printed here, but one set of manuscripts reads μὰ τὴν Ἥραν for μὰ τὸν Δία. As lectio difficilior, μὰ τὴν Ἥραν must surely be the correct reading. Swearing by Hera is typical of Socrates, but it also occasionally attested in other speakers, some apparently from the same deme as Socrates (cf. Sommerstein, CQ 58 [2008], forthcoming), but some apparently just displaying Socrates' influence on Xenophon's own speech and composition. If this reading is correct, it is a unique example of an invocation of Hera for a negative statement. All other examples read Νὴ τὴν Ἥραν. Cf. oath ids 1870, 1872, 2102, 2106, 2122, 2144, 2268, 2494.
