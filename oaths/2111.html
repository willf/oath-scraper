The gods    
 
  Remarks: Xenophon states that when the two forces reached the junction of the Tigris and Zapatas rivers, suspicion was rife, although no plot came to light.
When Clearchus next saw Tissaphernes, he addressed the issue, saying, "I know, Tissaphernes, that both of us have sworn oaths and given our right hands, not to injure one another, yet I see you are on your guard against us, and we, observing this, are keeping guard on our side".
Clearchus goes on to tell Tissaphernes that he is mistaken to mistrust the Greeks for two reasons:
Firstly, because they have consigned their friendship to the keeping of the gods. Clearchus says, "the oaths sworn by the gods stop us from being at war with each other, and the man who is conscious that he has disregarded such oaths I should never account happy."
He goes on to add, "if one is at war with the gods [for violating their oaths], I do not know by what swiftness of foot in trying to flee one could make his escape, or into what darkness he could stead away, or how he could withdraw himself into a secure fortress, for all places are subject to the gods."
Secondly, Clearchus says that on the human level (as opposed to that of the gods) he would not violate his oaths because Tissaphernes is the greatest good that they possess.
Ctesias refers to the breaking of this oath by Tissaphernes (see oath id 3485).
