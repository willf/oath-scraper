Oath ID 125: Euripides, Hippolytus, 611-612, (literary, Trag., 428)


  Date: mythical time
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Troezen   Location: unknown
 
  Swearer:
Hippolytus (male, adolescent, free, Athenian)
  Swearee:
Nurse (female, old, chattel-slave, n/a)
 
  Proposed by:
Nurse
 
  If taken: The Nurse will divulge her secret
  If refused: The Nurse will not divulge her secret
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: yes
  Impact: Hippolytus is bound against his will and will later be unable to clear his name
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features unknown
 
  Statement: [That he will not reveal what he has been told]
 
  Linguistic: omwm, ork, wmot
 
  God(s) invoked:
The gods    
 
  Remarks: Line 612, where Hippolytus states that his tongue has sworn but his heart isn't under oath, was famously parodied by Aristophanes- see oath ids 376, 514, 711. The oath is referred to several times during the play (see oath ids 126, 132, 133). Aristotle in his Rhetoric cites this passage with reference to Hygiaenon's accusation of Euripides' impiety (see oath id 1739). Socrates also refers to this passage during the Theaetetus when commenting on a seemingly paradoxical response (see oath id 2896). Compare also Bacchylides Pasiphae (?) 26.8 where Pasiphae makes Daedalus swear an oath of secrecy about her illness (i.e. lust for the bull)- see oath id 1342.
