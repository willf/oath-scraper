  Statement: [That perhaps it is clear that in certain collectivities it can't possibly be true that the many have more intelligence than the few]
εἰ μὲν οὖν περὶ πάντα δῆμον καὶ περὶ πᾶν πλῆθος ἐνδέχεται ταύτην εἶναι τὴν διαφορὰν τῶν πολλῶν πρὸς τοὺς ὀλίγους σπουδαίους, ἄδηλον, ἴσως δὲ νὴ Δία δῆλον ὅτι περὶ ἐνίων ἀδύνατον
 
  Linguistic: nh

Zeus    
 
  Remarks: The concept being discussed here is that the many have more intelligence than the few because there are more of them, and a collective judgement will be better than the judgement of a few regardless of intelligence. But this may not always be true, and in some cases it most certainly is not. For example, many animals will not have more intelligence than a few men - and, Aristotle adds, there are some humans who to all intents and purposes are no better than animals. However, he immediately goes on to say that a blanket rejection of the "wisdom of crowds" principle would be equally unjustified.
