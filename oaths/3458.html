Oath ID 3458: Callisthenes, Hellenica, Fr. 23.3 Jacoby, (literary, Hist., 350-336)


  Date: mythical history
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Arcadia, Messene   Location: unknown
 
  Swearer:
Aristocrates (male, mature, power-holder, Arcadian)
  Swearee:
Aristomenes (male, mature, power-holder, Messenian)
 
  Proposed by:
unknown
 
  If taken: Alliance between Messene and Arcadia would be formed
  If refused: Alliance between Messene and Arcadia would not be formed
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: no
  Impact: Alliance formalised
  Consequences of breach: He is stoned to death by his own people and flung beyond the borders of Arcadia without burial
 
  Sanctifying features unknown
 
  Statement: [That he would be a loyal ally of Messene]
 
  Linguistic: ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: The perjury mentioned here refers to the treachery of Aristocrates who betrayed his Messenian allies to the Spartans, in exchange for a bribe, at the Battle of the Great Trench in the First Messenian War (see Pausanias 4.17). It was only when Aristocrates tried to betray the Messenians to the Spartans for a second time that his original treachery was discovered. Pausanias (4.12) tells us that when this was revealed, the Arcadians themselves stoned Aristocrates to death and threw him out of their borders without burial. They had encouraged the Messenians to join in, but Aristomenes had reacted to the news with eyes fixed to the ground and weeping. So the Arcadians punished Aristocrates themselves. Pausanias says that it was they who set up a tablet in the precinct of Zeus Lycaeus which read: "Truly time has found justice for an unjust king and, with the help of Zeus, has easily found the betrayer of Messene. It is a difficult thing for a perjured man to escape the notice of god. Hail, king Zeus, and keep Arcadia safe". Polybius (4.33.3), however, who credits Callisthenes with reporting this inscription, says that the Messenians set it up on a pillar beside the altar of Zeus Lycaeus.
