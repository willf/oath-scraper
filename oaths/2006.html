Oath ID 2006: Aristotle, Titel 143,1 Gigon, 31.62, (literary, Phil., 335-322)


  Date: protohistorical time (?) (0)
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Corinth   Location: unknown
 
  Swearer:
Polemarchus (male, mature, free, Corinthian)
  Swearee:
Registrars of naval expedition (male, mature, free, Corinthian)
 
  Proposed by:
n/a
 
  If taken: He would be able to escape service in the expedition
  If refused: He would not be able to escape service in the expedition
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown (see remarks for punishment on perjury)
 
  Taken: yes   True: no
  Impact: He was relieved of his obligation to serve
  Consequences of breach: see remarks
 
  Sanctifying features unknown
 
  Statement: [That he was not in a position (could be financially or physically) to take part in the expedition]
 
  Linguistic: ork
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: This reference comes from extracts of Politeiai attributed to Heracleides (in this case of the Locrians), which Gigon regards as all derived from Aristotle but to which he gives no fragment numbers. The passage relates to a certain Polemarchus (or perhaps a holder of the office of polemarch) who, at some unspecified time, committed perjury and was able to escape service in the expedition of the Corinthians. He is subsequently plagued by weasels who bite him continually during the night when he tries to sleep, eventually causing him to take his own life.
