Oath ID 1916: Xenophon, Hellenica, 3.4.5-6, (literary, Hist., 385-354)


  Date: 396
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Ephesus   Location: unknown
 
  Swearer:
Tissaphernes (male, mature, power-holder, Persian)
  Swearee:
Herippidas (male, mature/old, power-holder, Spartan)
Dercylidas (male, mature/old, power-holder, Spartan)
Megillus (male, mature/old, power-holder, Spartan)
 
  Proposed by:
Tissaphernes
 
  If taken: The truce would be formalised
  If refused: There would not be a truce
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: more complex
  Impact: see remarks

  Statement: [That in very truth and guilelessly he would negotiate the peace]
ἦ μὴν πράξειν ἀδόλως τὴν εἰρήνην
 
  Linguistic: eirhn, speis, spond, wmos
 
  God(s) invoked:
unknown    
 
  Remarks: At Ephesus, Tissaphernes sent to Agesilaus and asked him with what intent he had come to Asia Minor. Agesilaus answered, "that the cities in Asia shall be independent, as are those in our part of Greece." Tissaphernes replied, "then if you are willing to make a truce until I can send to the King, I think you could accomplish this object and, if you should so desire, sail back home."
Agesilaus responded, "indeed I should so desire, if I could but think that I was not being deceived by you."
Tissaphernes reponded to this, "but it is possible for you to receive a guarantee on this point, that in very truth and guilelessly, if you follow this course, we will do no harm to any part of your domain during the truce."
After this agreement had been reached, Tissaphernes swore an oath to the representatives who were sent to him, that in very truth and guilelessly he would negotiate the peace, and they swore in turn on behalf of Agesilaus to Tissaphernes that in very truth, if he did this, Agesilaus would steadfastly observe the truce (see oath id 1917).
Xenophon observes that Tissaphernes straightway violated the oaths which he had sworn; for instead of keeping peace he sent to the King for a large army in addition to that which he had before. But Agesilaus, though he was aware of this, nevertheless continued to abide by the truce.
For the consequences of breach see 3.4.25 where Tithraustes says to Agesilaus, "the man who caused these troubles both to you and to us has got his deserts" after the King beheaded Tissaphernes in 395.
