  Remarks: Apollodorus states that he has entered a suit against Antiphanes because he did not testify or take the exomosia according to the law. We know from §19 that Antiphanes did not appear in court when summoned (he kept refusing to let Apollodorus submit his evidence to the arbitrator, always promising he’d testify on the day; when the day came, he didn’t turn up, even when Apollodorus went to his home to summon him; and the arbitrator, after waiting all day, gave a verdict against Apollodorus.)
Apollodorus demands that Antiphanes gets up and swears whether he loaned Timotheus 1000 drachmas in Calaureia, and whether Philip received that sum from Pasion.
Apollodorus presumably wants Antiphanes to do what he wouldn’t do before the arbitrator – either confirm that the money was indeed lent to Timotheus and that it originally belonged to Pasion, or deny it under oath. (There would surely be no need for an oath – certainly Apollodorus would have no interest in demanding one – if Antiphanes was willing to confirm these propositions.) And since at the end of §20 Apollodorus passes straight on to other matters, and never mentions Ant. again except briefly in passing at §48, we may safely take it that all this rigmarole about calling Antiphanes as a witness and demanding that he go up to the stand is just play-acting and that Apollodorus knows perfectly well that Antiphanes is not in court at all.
