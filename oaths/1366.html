  Remarks: Oaths by Hera are very unusual in poetry; cf. oath ids 135 and 187 in Euripides, though both these oaths are 'by the queen'- Hera's name is not invoked directly. Presumably the speaker (whether it’s the poetic I, or someone else) is really saying that they’ve got a reliable lock on their tongue, since a lock is what a treasure-house needs if it’s to be a trustworthy place to put treasure. A. H. Sommerstein, in correspondence, suggests that the fragment must be tragic for the following reasons: " it's dactylo-epitrite - which probably means it's Bacchylides, Pindar, or tragedy - and since SKHPTOUXON is an Ionic-Attic form, not choral Doric, tragedy is more likely (cf. Eur. IT 235). Another reason for suspecting it's tragedy, if any were needed, is that it's hard to imagine Pindar or Bacchylides proclaiming that they can be trusted to keep their mouths shut. This is surely a chorus, probably of women and probably Euripidean, swearing itself to secrecy in the way so many tragic choruses (mostly of women and mostly Euripidean) do; their choice of Hera to swear by may indicate that they are married, or that they are Argive, or (quite likely) both. For NAI (without MA) introducing an affirmative oath in Euripidean lyrics cf. Bacchae 534" - id 181.
