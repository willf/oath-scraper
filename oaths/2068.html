  Statement: [That he does indeed share his master's food]
τῷ δὲ κυνί, ὃς οὐδὲν τοιοῦτόν σοι παρέχει, μεταδίδως οὗπερ αὐτὸς ἔχεις σίτου. τὸν κύνα οὖν ἀκούσαντα εἰπεῖν· Ναὶ μὰ Δί’·

  Remarks: Socrates gives Aristarchus advice here illustrated by a fable. Aristarchus has been charged by the women of his household as "eating the bread of idleness". Socrates relates a fable in which a sheep comes to her master and asks why the dog gets to share the master's food when he gives none of what the sheep gives the master (wool, lambs and cheese). The dog overhears and replies that, of course, he shares his master's food for does he not protect the sheep from being eaten by wolves or stolen by thieves? So also Aristarchus should tell his women that he is their watch-dog.
