Oath ID 1176: Aristophanes, Lysistrata, 443-444, (literary, Comic., 411)


  Date: 411
  Reference type: Oath
  State: Athens   Location: Outside the Acropolis
 
  Swearer:
Second Old Woman (female, old, free, Athenian)
  Swearee:
Scythian archer (male, mature, chattel-slave, n/a)
 
  Proposed by:
n/a
 
  If taken: n/a
  If refused: n/a
  If kept: unknown
  If broken: unknown
 
  Taken: yes   True: yes
  Impact: Emphasises the statement
  Consequences of breach: n/a
 
  Sanctifying features Acropolis setting
 
  Statement: [That if the archer touches her even with the tip of his finger, he will soon he begging for a cupping-vessel]
Εἰ τἄρα νὴ τὴν Φωσφόρον τὴν χεῖρ’ ἄκραν
ταύτῃ προσοίσεις, κύαθον αἰτήσεις τάχα.
 
  Linguistic: nh thn
 
  God(s) invoked:
Hecate    
 
  Remarks: The second old woman here reinforces the oaths sworn by both Lysistrata and the the first old woman (see ids 1174 and 1175) that whoever lays a finger on Lysistrata will regret it. Hecate is also called 'Light-Bringer' in Aristophanes at Thesm. 858 and fr. 608 K-A. Sommerstein ad 444 explains the phrase "you'll soon be begging for a cupping vessel" as follows: "i.e. [he] will get a black eye… and want to use a kyathos, a bronze vessel that could be applied to the skin for the relief of bruises and swellings".
